> Course Reviews


Below are a list of courses I have taken during my time at Carnegie Mellon University. I included a brief review of each course, what I liked and disliked about the course, and any other general comments regarding the course material. I hope this helps you in your course selection process! Courses that I thought were particularly interesting are marked with ✭, and courses that I think makes CMU worth attending are marked with ❤️.


21-127: Concepts of Mathematics

Professor(s): Clive Newstead

CMU's 21-127 was my first introduction to formal mathematics and proof writing. The course provided me with new perspectives on discrete math and reshaped my view of mathematics as a formal logical system. I found the transition to this formal style challenging but rewarding as it deepened my mathematical reasoning skills. Clive Newstead was an amazing lecturer. During his office hours, he would often go into the history of theorems and proofs, which I found fascinating. Though this course is required for most STEM majors at CMU, I would highly recommend non-STEM majors to take it with Clive since it provides a new perspective on mathematics.


15-122: Principles of Imperative Computation

Professor(s): Iliano Cervesato, Dilsun Kaynar

15-122 was my first computer science class at CMU. It teaches you how to write provably safe and correct programs in a subset of C called C0. The course is very difficult to get an A in as a freshman since a lot of the material is new and it is designed to be challenging. Regardless, I found the course to be rewarding as it taught me how to implement the data structures and algorithms used in the industry in a bare-bones language. The programming assignments are actually interesting, and the final project for the course even requires you to implement a virtual machine in C. Overall, I think 15-122 is a great introduction to computer science, and has the added benefit of making you a well-versed in C right off the bat.

P.S. I am currently TAing this course, so I am a bit biased. It's still a great course to take, though! I think this along with 21-127/21-128/15-151 are CMU's must take courses for anyone, not just STEM majors. Functions are pointers!


15-150: Principles of Functional Programming ✭

Professor(s): Micheal Erdmann, Karl Crary

15-150 is CMU's introduction to functional programming course. It is taught in Standard ML, and teaches you how to write provably correct programs using the functional programming paradigm. Unlike 15-122, 15-150 is quite easy to get an A if you take it with Erdmann and Crary. The programming assignments are challenging, but the exams are very reasonable. The course gave me a new perspective on programming, one that I think is very useful for writing correct code. For example, it's interesting to see that since every SML function is recursive, we can prove correctness of our programs using induction. Some of the proofs are very elegant, especially ones relating to functors and modules. I attended Crary's lectures, and he was amazing. Some people thought he went too fast, but that's why I liked him. He was very clear and concise, and his explanations were better than Erdmann's. 15-150 is overall an excellent course, and I would highly recommend it to anyone interested in computer science. Functions are values!


02-251: Great Ideas in Computational Biology ❤️

Professor(s): Phillip Compeau

02-251 made me fall in love with Computational Biology, and is one of the main reasons I am working at Mohimani Lab right now. Essentially, the course is a survey of all the pivotal algorithms that gave birth to the field of Computational Biology. From genome sequencing, to systems biology, to even neural networks, 02-251 covers it all. It is a theory heavy class as a lot of the algorithms discussed make use of advanced techniques such as dynamic programming and graph theory. Be prepared to do a lot of proofs about graphs and trees, but also get ready to implement a lot of algorithms from scratch. From the Needleman-Wunsch alignment algorithm to creating multi layer perceptrons, this course will teach you how to implement the most infuential algorithms in Computational Biology. Phillip is an excellent lecturer, and his passion for the subject is evident in his lectures. He is also very approachable and is always willing to help. Although the official pre-reqs for this class are only 15-112 and 21-127/21-128/15-151, I would recommend taking 15-122 either before or concurrently since it will help you with the programming assignments. Whether you are a computer scientist, biologist, chemist, or mathematician, 02-251 is a must take course.
